Hardware Development - I&O

Shortly after, Hien Minh joined the hardware development, focusing mainly on the keyboardâ€”one of the two hardest components. We needed something entirely new: a keyboard that functioned normally but could dynamically change values like a chameleon.

February 2025 - May 31, 2025

Hardware Development - PCB

Another major and by far the most headache-inducing problem was the PCB. We set extremely high goals for the hardware: low cost, rich in features, and compact in size. All of these requirements placed enormous pressure on the PCB design.

Still, I worked through it bit by bit, learning impedance and signal integrity along the way, redesigning everything again and again, sometimes deleting the entire project and starting from scratch. By the final stage, the PCB had evolved into something close to state-of-the-art: a six-layer design with extremely dense component packing, consisting of one main board, one power board, and several auxiliary boards. In total, it included over 355 individual components and more than ten A3 pages of schematics.

By reverse engineering existing products and studying plenty of open-source designs, I managed to speed up the process somewhat while still developing parts of the circuitry myself. I can confidently say this is one of the most ridiculous things I have ever built with my own hands.

However, due to limited experience, time, and budget, we ran into serious issues with the memory network, which ultimately stalled the project. The lack of proper simulation and our still-developing design standards also made the board vulnerable to regulatory violations, overheating, and stability problems.

Even so, this remains something I am genuinely impressed by and proud of.
